Research Areas: Computational Biology & Statistics for Single Cell Genomics in Human Diseases

The Gao laboratory at the Center of Bioinformatics and Computational Biology harbors both single cell sequencing technologies and computational methodologies under one roof. Dr. Gao has significant contribution in single cell sequencing technology, computational methodology and their applications. She developed the punctuated copy number evolution model in primary triple-negative breast cancer that is a paradigm shift challenging the Darwinian Gradualism in tumor evolution ( Nature Genetics, 2016), the bi-mode chemoresistance evolution model where adaptive selection of genotypes and inductive reprogramming of phenotypes are activated in parallel (Cell, 2018), the high throughput nanogrid single nuclear RNA sequencing method that opened the avenue of analyzing single cell transcriptomes from archival frozen tissue samples (Nature Communications, 2017) and the computational methodology for analyzing multi-layers of single cell genomics data (Nature Biotechnology, 2020). Moving forward, Gao Lab is focusing on developing novel computational tools to integrate single cell multi-omics and establishing new experimental pipelines to perform third generation of single cell long read and spatial sequencing to unveil both somatic and mosaic genetic abnormalities that underly human cancer, vascular and muscular diseases.

Bioinformatic methodologies for analyzing single cell sequencing data


We are dedicated to developing novel computational tools for analyzing single cell sequencing data to understand the underlying mechanisms of genetic predispositions in human diseases.

Single Cell Sequencing Technology


This effort involves the integration of diverse single cell sequencing technology and spatial imaging approaches as well as long read methods to obtain single cell multi-omics data from human tissues.

Brain Metastasis Evolution by Single Cell Multi-omics


We investigate the complex adaptive evolution models during brain metastasis by single cell multi-omics.
